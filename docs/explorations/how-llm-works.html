a plain-language walkthrough

How does an LLM
actually answer you?

It never writes the full answer in one shot. It goes step by step — predicting a single next word, again and again, until a sentence appears. Here's the whole pipeline, one station at a time.

“What is gravity?”
→
the model has not answered yet
text→ tokens→ token IDs→ vectors→ layers→ probabilities→ next token→ repeat→ answer
01

The model receives your text

At this point it's just ordinary human language — no numbers, no math yet. Just the words you typed.

What is gravity?
02

The text is broken into tokens

A token can be a whole word, part of a word, or a symbol. Long or unusual words often get split into pieces.

"What is gravity?"  →  split by the tokenizer
What is grav ity ?
03

Each token becomes a number

Models can't read letters directly. Every token is looked up in a vocabulary and swapped for a token ID. The sentence is now just a list of numbers.

What4213
is318
grav2489
ity1097
?30
04

Numbers become vectors of meaning

A vector is a long list of numbers that represents meaning, not just identity. It's why the model knows "gravity" sits close to force, mass, earth, and physics — and far from unrelated ideas.

gravity  →  [ 0.82, -0.15, 0.44, -0.06, ... ]
05

Vectors pass through transformer layers

Think of each layer as one round of thinking. Every round asks: which words matter here, which tokens are related, what context should I remember? This is attention — it helps the model focus on the right parts of the input.

Layer 1
"Which tokens are related?"
Layer 2
"What context should I remember?"
Layer 3
"Which words matter here?"
Layer N
… many more rounds
06

A final internal understanding forms

After all those layers, the model has a rich internal picture of the prompt. It still hasn't written anything — it has only prepared the context.

context ready,
no answer written yet
07

The model predicts the next token

It scores every token in its vocabulary and assigns each one a probability. After "Gravity is a", most of the probability piles onto a small handful of sensible words.

force
72%
concept
19%
banana
1%
08

A sampling method chooses the token

Sometimes the single most likely token wins. Sometimes the model draws from a small pool of likely candidates — which is why the same prompt can produce slightly different answers each time.

“force” selected
mint = force  ·  sky = concept  ·  grey = everything else
09

The token is added — then it repeats

The chosen token joins the output, and the entire process runs again to pick the next one. That's why answers appear on screen word by word.

Gravity is
Gravity is a
Gravity is a force
Gravity is a force that…
↻ back to step 7, one token at a time

The full pipeline, start to finish

Nine quiet steps, repeated over and over, are what turn "What is gravity?" into a real answer.

text→ tokens→ token IDs→ vectors→ processing layers→ probabilities→ next token→ repeat→ final answer

Why "performance" isn't just about the model

This whole loop is called inference. How well it runs depends on more than the model's size or training.
